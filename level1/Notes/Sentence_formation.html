1. What is a Sentence?

A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought.

Features:

  • Must have a subject (who/what the sentence is about)
  • Must have a predicate/verb (what the subject does)
  • Starts with a capital letter and ends with . ? !
Ram is reading a book.
Are you coming?
Wow! This is amazing.

2. Parts of a Sentence

2.1 Subject

The subject tells who or what the sentence is about.

She runs fast.
The dog barked loudly.

2.2 Predicate (Verb/Action)

The predicate tells what the subject does or is.

She runs fast.
The dog barked loudly.

2.3 Object (Optional)

The object receives the action.

He eats an apple.
She is reading a book.

2.4 Complement (Optional)

Gives more information about the subject or object.

She is a teacher.
He seems happy.

2.5 Adverbs/Adjectives/Other modifiers

Provide extra detail about how, when, where, or what kind.

She runs very fast.
He bought a red car.

3. Basic Sentence Structure (S + V + O)

The simplest sentence follows this order: Subject + Verb + Object

I (S) eat (V) an apple (O).
Ram (S) is reading (V) a book (O).
She (S) loves (V) chocolate (O).

Note: Not all sentences have an object.

She sleeps. (No object)

4. Types of Sentences (Based on Function)

4.1 Declarative Sentence

Statement that gives information. Ends with .

I am a student.
He plays football.

4.2 Interrogative Sentence

Question. Ends with ?

Are you coming?
What is your name?

4.3 Imperative Sentence

Command, request, or instruction. Ends with . or !

Close the door.
Please sit down.
Stop!

4.4 Exclamatory Sentence

Expresses strong emotion. Ends with !

Wow! That‚Äôs amazing.
Hurray! We won the match.

5. Sentence Formation Rules

  1. Every sentence must have a subject and a verb.
  2. Modifiers (adjectives/adverbs) can enhance but cannot replace subject or verb.
  3. Word order matters:
    Correct: She (S) eats (V) an apple (O).
    Wrong: Eats she an apple.
  4. Use punctuation correctly: . ? !
  5. Agreement: Subject and verb must agree in number and person.
    She is happy. (singular)
    They are happy. (plural)

10. Practice Tips

  • Start with simple S+V+O sentences
  • Use different types of subjects and verbs
  • Add adjectives and adverbs to improve sentence richness
  • Combine sentences using conjunctions
  • Read sentences aloud to practice flow

11. Final Summary

  • Every sentence expresses a complete thought
  • Must have subject and verb
  • Objects, complements, and modifiers add meaning
  • Punctuation, word order, and agreement are key
Mastering sentence formation will make your English clear, structured, and ready for fluent communication.
